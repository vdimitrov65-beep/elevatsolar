So why are some EPC offers still suspicious?

The Critical Distinction

Two types of lower prices. Only one is legitimate.

The 87% TIC reduction happened because modules got better, inverters got smarter, and supply chains scaled globally. That's value creation. The suspicious offers appearing in Romania and Bulgaria today are driven by something else entirely.

"Lower installed costs driven by technology are a global trend. Lower EPC offers driven by contractor desperation are a different story — and the bill doesn't arrive at COD."

— Elevat Solar, from on-the-ground experience in SEE, 2025–2026

Module-level quality has also deteriorated at scale. The 2024 Kiwa PVEL PV Module Reliability Scorecard found that two-thirds of manufacturers experienced at least one test failure — the highest percentage ever recorded. Bill of Materials failure rates reached 41%, up from 26% in 2021. Clean Energy Associates documented a 47% increase in micro-crack incidence from early 2022, a period of explosive module production scale-up.

The implication for SEE procurement: a contractor buying on price, without independent factory audits or pre-shipment inspection, is absorbing this quality lottery into your project.

What Suspicious Offers Actually Look Like

The bill arrives at year 3–5, not at COD

A €200/kW gap on a 50 MWp project is €10 million. That money is recovered somewhere in the execution — typically across five predictable compromise points:

⚡
Cable sizing "optimised" beyond reason

Undersized DC strings and AC cables to save copper cost. Manifests as chronic voltage drop and elevated losses — undocumented, unwarranted, permanent.

🏗️
Civil works executed in winter

Concrete poured below minimum temperature thresholds, piling without proper frost depth. Cracks visible by summer 1. Structural remediation cost exceeds the saving.

📋
Copy-paste commissioning reports

String-level IV curve data from a different project, or from simulation only. Impossible to trigger performance guarantees or warranty claims without baseline data.

🔍
No independent monitoring at string level

SCADA covers inverters but not strings. Underperforming strings go undetected for months. In a 50 MWp plant, a 3% string loss across 40 tables costs €80–120k annually.

📦
Module procurement without factory audit

No pre-shipment inspection, no EL testing, no power bin validation. The contractor passes your capital risk to a manufacturer you never approved.

✓
What bankable execution looks like

IE appointed by lender, independent QA at construction milestones, as-built documentation at string level, PR ≥ 80% warranty on P90 basis, 5–10% retention 12 months post-COD.

What bankable projects actually cost in SEE today

Based on Elevat Solar's advisory portfolio (400+ MWp across Romania and Bulgaria), a properly specified utility-scale project in SEE — with independent engineering, bankable equipment, and complete documentation — comes in at €680–780/kW all-in. This includes:

Minimum Bankability Checklist · SEE 2025/2026

If your EPC offer doesn't cover this, ask what's missing

  • →Independent Engineer (IE) scope embedded in EPC contract, lender-approved
  • →Module supply with factory audit or pre-shipment inspection by independent lab
  • →As-built documentation at string level — not inverter level
  • →IV curve commissioning data per string, not simulation output
  • →Performance ratio warranty ≥ 80% on P90 basis, with measurement protocol defined
  • →Retention 5–10% held for minimum 12 months post-COD
  • →Tracker installation in non-frozen ground with documented soil bearing test
  • →Supply chain traceability per EU Solar Charter / SolarPower Europe guidelines

The bottom line: The 87% cost decline since 2010 was earned by engineers and manufacturers. The suspicious offers appearing in SEE today are not a continuation of that trend — they are a transfer of risk from contractor to asset owner, deferred in time and hidden in commissioning reports. Procurement decisions based on headline EPC price without technical due diligence are not cost savings. They are deferred liabilities.
